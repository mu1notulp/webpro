Coral Restoration in the Pacific Islands

Why Coral Restoration?

Ocean Acidification

The NOAA, which stands for National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, are establishing efforts worldwide to replant our corals, many of which have bleached themselves and died. Over half of the world's reefs have been lost since 1970. Mass coral bleaching events are a major principle in these happenings.


What is Coral Bleaching?

Why does it happen?

Coral bleaching is triggered when symbiotic algae expels from the coral as a stress reaction due to changes in the coral's environment. This is done when sea temperatures elevate. This change stresses the coral, as they expel the algae, which is crucial in providing energy through photosynthesis and give corals their signature pop of color, the coral loses it's color and is rendered vulnerable. If the surrounding conditions do not get better, the coral is unable to recover, and as a result, dies, as there is only so much algae it can expel to protect itself. This severly affects the biodiversity of oceanic ecosystems and the environment.


What is Ocean Acidification?

What also causes coral bleaching.

In addition to pollution, rising sea temperatures, and disease. Ocean acidification has proven to be a significant threat to the livelihood of coral reefs and environments. Ocean acidification is a process that occurs when carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere is absorbed by seawater, leading to a decrease in pH levels and making the ocean more acidic. This phenomenon is primarily driven by human activities, such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation, which increase CO2 concentrations in the atmosphere. For corals, ocean acidification poses significant challenges. Corals rely on calcium carbonate to build their skeletons, a process that becomes more difficult in acidic conditions. Lower pH levels reduce the availability of carbonate ions, which are essential for coral calcification. As a result, corals may grow more slowly or even begin to dissolve, leading to weakened structures that are more vulnerable to stressors like temperature changes, storms, and disease.

Why are corals important?

Coral reefs are vital ecosystems that play a crucial role in marine biodiversity. They provide habitat and shelter for a diverse range of marine species, including fish, invertebrates, and other organisms. Covering only about 0.1% of the ocean floor, coral reefs support approximately 25% of all marine life, making them essential for maintaining the health of ocean ecosystems. In addition to their ecological significance, coral reefs offer substantial economic benefits. They contribute to the livelihoods of millions of people through fisheries, tourism, and coastal protection. Healthy reefs protect shorelines from erosion and storm surges, reducing the impact of natural disasters on coastal communities. Coral reefs are also important for scientific research, offering insights into climate change, marine biology, and ecosystem health. However, they are facing severe threats from climate change, pollution, and overfishing, leading to widespread coral bleaching and degradation. Protecting and restoring coral reefs is essential not only for marine biodiversity but also for human well-being and economic stability.

Coral Restoration

Coral gardening and rehabilitation

Replanting corals, often referred to as coral restoration, involves several key techniques aimed at rehabilitating damaged reefs. One common method is coral gardening, where fragments of healthy coral are collected and grown in nurseries. These nurseries can be underwater or on land, depending on the species and environmental conditions. Once the corals reach a certain size and maturity, they are carefully transplanted back onto degraded reef areas. Another technique is direct seeding, where coral larvae are cultivated in a lab and then released onto damaged reefs. This method helps enhance the natural recruitment of corals in areas that have lost their populations. Additionally, researchers often use microfragmentation, where larger coral pieces are broken into smaller fragments to encourage rapid growth and increase the chances of survival when transplanted. Monitoring and maintenance are crucial after replanting to ensure the success of restoration efforts. This includes assessing the health of the transplanted corals and managing surrounding environmental conditions, such as water quality and marine life interactions. Through these methods, coral restoration projects aim to rebuild resilient ecosystems and support the recovery of coral reefs in the face of ongoing threats.


Deforestation Efforts to Mitigate Climate Change

Why Deforestation Mitigation?

Our Oxygen

We require oxygen to keep our bodies functioning, and almost about 30% of our oxygen is derived from forests and trees. Unfortunately, due to mass deforestation, we've lost so much of our forests and vital resource land. Deforestation mitigation efforts are vital for several interconnected reasons. First, forests are home to approximately 80% of the world's terrestrial biodiversity, so protecting and restoring these ecosystems is essential for preserving habitats for countless species, including many that are endangered. Additionally, forests play a crucial role in carbon sequestration by absorbing significant amounts of CO2 from the atmosphere, which helps combat climate change. Moreover, forests contribute to the regulation of the water cycle, influencing precipitation patterns and water quality. Their loss can lead to soil erosion, reduced water availability, and increased flooding. Healthy forests also stabilize soil, preventing erosion and maintaining nutrient content, which is critical for agricultural productivity. Many communities, particularly Indigenous peoples, rely on forests for their livelihoods, food, and cultural identity. Sustainable forest management can provide economic opportunities without compromising environmental health. Furthermore, healthy forests contribute to clean air and water, while deforestation can exacerbate health issues by increasing air pollution and reducing access to clean water sources. Finally, forests provide resilience against climate impacts, acting as buffers that help communities adapt to changing environmental conditions. Overall, mitigating deforestation is essential not only for environmental sustainability but also for economic stability and social equity, leading to healthier ecosystems and communities.
